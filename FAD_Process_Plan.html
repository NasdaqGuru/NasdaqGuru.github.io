How Phase 3 Works With FAD’s Eight Reporting Units

Hub-and-spoke operating model

Phase 3 does not replace existing FAD reporting units. It gives them a shared implementation layer: OFA and the Controller set direction and governance; each reporting unit contributes subject-matter standards; Tub Finance Directors translate the change locally; departmental users receive practical training; and the dashboard tracks whether adoption is sustained after launch.

Set direction

OFA, Controller, and FAD leadership confirm the standard, exception policy, value target, and adoption threshold.

Shape content

Relevant reporting units define the subject-matter rules, data definitions, workflow controls, and training requirements.

Translate locally

Tub Finance Directors map the standard to school operations, identify legitimate local exceptions, and sponsor local readiness.

Train and adopt

OFA training partners, super users, and process owners deliver role-based learning, office hours, and go-live support.

Sustain and improve

Dashboards monitor adoption, cycle time, exceptions, user experience, and whether old workarounds return.

Phase 3 principle

Implementation is not a communications blast; it is a managed behavior-change program. Each stakeholder group needs a different reason to believe, a different training pathway, a different support model, and a different success measure. OFA should be positioned as a trusted enablement partner, not only a policy messenger.
Comprehensive Change Management & Communication Matrix
Central FAD Leadership
Controller, FAD senior leaders, process executives, policy owners, systems sponsors
Executive sponsor path
Likely resistance to standardization
  • Concern that standardization could create new operational risk during close, audit, budget, or fiscal year-end cycles.
  • Skepticism that benefits are measurable enough to justify leadership time and change disruption.
  • Concern that dashboards may expose gaps and create blame rather than improvement.
  • Competing strategic priorities across finance, systems, controls, and service delivery.
Key buy-in message
“This is a fiduciary and service-quality upgrade.”
Standardization gives leadership one reliable view of cycle time, exception rates, control quality, administrative burden, and user experience. The ask of senior leaders is to sponsor the standard, remove barriers, and reinforce the same message across the enterprise.
Training delivery with OFA
Executive briefingsController decision labsGovernance simulationsKPI dashboard walkthroughsSponsor toolkit
  • Monthly 45-minute leadership labs focused on decisions, not technical navigation.
  • OFA co-branded sponsor talking points, escalation scripts, and decision-rights guide.
Tub Finance Directors
School and tub finance leads, local finance directors, senior administrators, business office managers
Local flexibility concern
Likely resistance to standardization
  • Fear that “central standard” means loss of local flexibility or school-specific nuance.
  • Concern that implementation burden will fall on already stretched local finance teams.
  • Past experience with tools that changed reporting requirements without reducing workload.
  • Risk that local exception handling, legacy spreadsheets, and workarounds remain faster in the short term.
Key buy-in message
“Standardize the common 80%; preserve approved local exceptions for the true 20%.”
The goal is not to flatten schools. It is to reduce rework, make exceptions visible, give tub leaders a stronger voice in implementation, and protect legitimate local requirements through a formal exception pathway.
Training delivery with OFA
Train-the-trainer cohortTub readiness workbookPeer implementation forumsScenario workshopsOffice hours
  • Two 90-minute cohort sessions per tub: readiness + live process simulation.
  • OFA-supported “exception vs. standard” decision guide for local leaders.
Departmental Transaction Processors
Front-line staff entering, reviewing, correcting, routing, or reconciling transactions
Daily workflow focus
Likely resistance to standardization
  • Concern that the new process will slow down daily work at peak periods.
  • Uncertainty about new terminology, required fields, approval paths, and system screens.
  • Fear of making mistakes or being held responsible for errors during transition.
  • Preference for familiar local workarounds that feel faster even when they create downstream rework.
Key buy-in message
“This should make your daily work easier and reduce rejected transactions.”
The new standard should provide one way to submit, one checklist to follow, clearer approval ownership, fewer returned items, and visible support when something does not fit the standard path.
Training delivery with OFA
Hands-on sandboxMicrolearning videosQuick-reference guidesWalk-up clinicsSuper-user network
  • 45–60 minute task-based sessions by transaction type.
  • OFA co-branded “Before you submit” checklists and 5-minute correction videos.

What the Data Means for the Controller — Plain Language

📊 Policy Compliance at 84%

84 out of every 100 financial transactions follow the approved Harvard policy and contract framework. The remaining 16% are "maverick" — processed outside approved channels. This is up significantly from 66% a year ago, largely due to the P2P Streamlining and Budget Transfer Redesign initiatives. The target is 90%.

The School with lowest compliance is FAS (71%). A targeted compliance intervention for FAS business officers is recommended for Q4 FY2026.

⚠️ 8.4% Exception Rate

An "exception" is when a financial transaction doesn't pass automated validation and needs a human to review it. Think of it like a TSA security flag — most turn out to be fine, but every one takes time and creates audit exposure. At 8.4%, we're processing about 8 exceptions for every 100 transactions. The goal is under 5%.

The highest exception rate is in Travel & Expense (14.2%) — Concur is rolling out now to address this.

🔍 2.1% Data Discrepancy Rate

This measures how often the data in our financial systems doesn't match across Oracle, Jaggaer, and reporting dashboards — like a bank statement that doesn't reconcile. At 2.1%, down from 9.4%, our data is now reliable enough for audit-quality reporting. The target is under 2%.

Two open governance incidents (unauthorized access and format anomaly) should be reviewed before the Q4 audit package is finalized.

⚖️ 2 Policy Escalations Pending

There are two active policy questions that need a formal Controller decision: (1) Delegation of Authority Modernization — current thresholds are misaligned with new P2P workflow approvals; and (2) P-Card Threshold & Controls Update — raised during P2P streamlining. Both drafts are ready for review.

These decisions are time-sensitive: P-Card controls must be finalized before P2P Phase 2 go-live in August 2026.

Plain-Language Guide — Understanding What's Changing

🔄 What Is Financial Process Transformation?

Think of it like a kitchen renovation. The food (your financial work) stays the same, but we're replacing old, inefficient equipment with modern tools. Some things will feel different at first — new ways to approve purchases, new forms to submit expenses, new reports to use. The goal is to save you time, reduce errors, and make your financial work feel less like navigating a maze.

📊 What Does "Adoption Rate" Mean?

Your school's adoption rate tells us how many of your finance staff are successfully using the new processes and tools — not just trained, but actually working the new way. Think of it like a new coffee machine in the office: 72% adoption means 72 out of 100 people have switched to the new machine. The remaining 28% are still using the old workarounds.

⏱️ Why Do Cycle Times Matter?

A "cycle time" is how long it takes to complete one financial process from start to finish — like how long it takes from submitting a purchase request to getting the actual purchase order. Shorter cycle times mean your faculty and staff wait less, projects move faster, and your team spends less time chasing approvals. The goal isn't speed for its own sake — it's eliminating the unnecessary waiting.

🆘 How to Get Help

If your staff are struggling with a new process, or something isn't working as expected, there are three ways to get help: (1) Your School Finance Transformation Lead — the first point of contact; (2) The FAD Help Desk — for system-specific issues; (3) The Monthly Finance Community of Practice — peer learning sessions where schools share what's working.

Best Practices — How High-Performing Transformation Leaders Stay on Track

🗓️ Weekly "5-Minute Flag Review"

Every Monday, spend 5 minutes reviewing the Action Center for any new flags or status changes. Best practice from McKinsey transformation research: directors who review their flag list weekly are 3× more likely to catch resistance signals before they become critical escalations. Set a calendar block — it prevents surprises.

🎯 "Red Item Rule" — No More Than 3

If your Action Center has more than 3 red flags at any time, you have more critical issues than a Director can actively manage simultaneously. Best practice: when red flags exceed 3, call an emergency steering committee within 5 business days to either resolve, delegate, or formally defer lower-priority items. Three is the cognitive limit for sustained high-attention management.

📊 "Data Before Story" — Executive Communication

When briefing the Controller or CFO on transformation progress, lead with the three numbers that most directly answer "Is it working?" — current exception rate, admin hours reclaimed, and adoption rate. Research from Harvard Business Review shows that executive briefings leading with outcomes (not activities) are 40% more likely to receive funding continuation decisions. Then narrate.

🤝 "Resistance Early Warning" — Monthly Pulse

Best practice: send a 3-question monthly pulse to all school Finance Directors: (1) Is there anything about the transformation that is causing problems for your team? (2) What one thing could we do differently? (3) What is working well? Directors who run this pulse catch resistance 6–8 weeks earlier than those who rely on formal steering committee feedback alone. Low cost, high signal value.
